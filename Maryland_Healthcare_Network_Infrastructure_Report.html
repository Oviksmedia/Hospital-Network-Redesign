1 Executive Summary

The hospital's internet and Wi-Fi infrastructure was suffering from chronic instability caused by a Triple DHCP conflict, fragmented network architecture, and degraded physical cabling. The entire network has been systematically redesigned, reconfigured, and physically rewired to deliver a centralized, stable, and high-performance infrastructure.

The primary beneficiaries of this upgrade are the clinical staff. Doctors and nurses utilize two dedicated tablets for ward rounds, and previously struggled with network issues as they moved between areas. With the new centralized infrastructure and seamless roaming, these tablets maintain a continuous, fast connection anywhere in the facility.

The project was completed over the course of three working days, including approximately 6+ hours of physical ceiling/roof work involving cable routing, retrieval of abandoned cabling, extension of critical network drops to new office locations, and replacement of faulty hardware connectors.

2 Pre-Existing Problems Identified

2.1 Triple DHCP Conflict CRITICAL

Three separate routers were simultaneously acting as independent DHCP servers, each racing to assign IP addresses to connecting devices. This impacted staff systems heavily, causing frequent Wi-Fi disconnections, IP address conflicts resulting in devices going offline, and the need for constant manual router reboots. Clinical staff rounding the wards experienced major disruptions when using hospital tablets.

Device Model Problem
TP-Link Archer C80 AC1900 MU-MIMO Running as DHCP Server #1
TP-Link TL-WR940N Wireless N 450Mbps Running as DHCP Server #2
TP-Link TL-MR3420 3G/4G Wireless N Running as DHCP Server #3

2.2 Double/Triple NAT

Multiple routers performing Network Address Translation created layered NAT chains, causing slower speeds, failed remote access attempts to the NVR camera system, and degraded application performance.

2.3 Fragmented Wi-Fi Networks

Each router was broadcasting a different, unrelated SSID (e.g., MARYLAND WIFI, Maryland Healthcare, MHC Exten, MD Office, Maryland Guest). Clinical staff had to manually switch Wi-Fi networks as they moved between different areas of the hospital, which interrupted clinical workflow during ward rounds.

2.4 Degraded Physical Cabling

Ethernet cables throughout the ceiling infrastructure were found to have faulty crimps, a defective coupler in the ceiling severely degrading signal quality, and multiple abandoned idle cables creating clutter and confusion.

2.5 Aging Equipment

The Cisco Catalyst 2960 switch (manufactured June 2007) is 19 years old and running end-of-life firmware. It functions as a basic Layer 2 switch but receives no security patches.

3.2 Network Architecture Redesign

The entire network topology was redesigned around a single-gateway, centralized DHCP architecture. All downstream routers were converted to transparent Access Points, and a unified Wi-Fi network was deployed across the entire facility.

After: One gateway managing all IP assignments, four Access Points broadcasting a single unified network.

3.5 Physical Infrastructure Work (Ceiling/Roof — 6+ Hours)

This was the most physically demanding portion of the project, involving extended work in the hospital's ceiling cavity and roof space.

Cable Routing & Extension
Extended the TL-WR940N network drop and the NVR (camera recorder) connection to the new Managing Director's office location, routing new Ethernet cable through the ceiling infrastructure. All cables terminated using the T568B wiring standard on both ends.
Cable Retrieval & Cleanup
Discovered and retrieved multiple idle/abandoned Ethernet cables throughout the ceiling that were no longer connected to any equipment. Removed unused cable runs to reduce clutter, prevent confusion during future maintenance, and recover reusable cable stock.
Troubleshooting the Historically Slowest Router (10 Mbps to 153 Mbps)
Investigated the network branch serving the TL-MR3420, which had historically been the slowest router at the facility. Even after converting the MR3420 to an Access Point, performance remained unusually poor. Conducted a direct wired test by connecting the AP's supply cable directly into a diagnostic laptop, revealing a severe 10 Mbps speed bottleneck. Traced the cable run into the ceiling and identified a defective Ubiquiti PoE coupler/brick that was severely degrading the signal (2 wires dead, 1 wire weak). Replaced the defective coupler with a clean, new RJ45 standard coupler and restored the connection. Result: Network speed on this specific cable run experienced a 15× speed increase — jumping from 10 Mbps to 153 Mbps directly, and restoring the MR3420 Access Point to its maximum 91+ Mbps wireless throughput.
Crimping & Connector Termination
Terminated all new and re-made cable runs with fresh RJ45 connectors. Overcame crimping tool difficulties through persistence and careful technique to achieve proper wire-to-pin contact on all connectors.

5 Recommendations

5.1 Immediate Actions (Within 1 Week)

Priority
  • Assign a static IP to the Hikvision NVR (e.g., 192.168.0.20) to prevent the remote camera viewing app from losing connectivity after power fluctuations or router reboots.
  • Verify remote camera access by testing the Hik-Connect app over cellular data (4G/5G) to confirm the NVR is correctly routing through the new gateway.

5.2 Short-Term (Within 1 Month)

  • Document all static IP assignments in an internal IT reference sheet stored in a secure, accessible location.
  • Re-crimp any remaining suspect cables using cable tester verification on all 8 wires before deployment.

5.3 Long-Term (3–6 Months)

  • Plan replacement of the Cisco Catalyst 2960 — the switch is 19 years old with end-of-life firmware. A modern managed switch would provide VLAN support, monitoring, and security.
  • Consider upgrading the TL-WR940N and TL-MR3420 to Wi-Fi 5 or Wi-Fi 6 Access Points. Both are limited to 2.4GHz / 100Mbps ports, restricting throughput in their coverage areas.
  • Implement network segmentation (VLANs) to isolate camera traffic, staff devices, and guest devices for security and performance.
